%% ========================= ABSTRACT ======================================= %%
\begin{abstract}
The forthcoming sixth-generation (6G) New Radio (NR) standard introduces
a suite of user equipment (UE) power-saving primitives, including
Bandwidth Part (BWP)-lite operation, Wake-Up Receiver (\wur{}) signalling,
and flexible Synchronisation Signal Block (\ssb{}) periodicity.
However, the static, rule-based configurations specified in current 3GPP
study items cannot adapt to heterogeneous traffic patterns and dynamic
channel conditions, leaving significant energy savings unrealised.
In this paper, we propose a Dueling Deep Q-Network (DQN) agent that
jointly optimises the BWP switching mode, \wur{} detection threshold,
and \ssb{} transmission period at the UE, formulated as a Markov Decision
Process (MDP) with a reward function that captures the fundamental
trade-off between power saving and quality-of-service (QoS).
The \wur{} threshold selection is grounded in a statistical energy detector
model, with miss-detection and false-alarm probabilities derived as
functions of instantaneous channel quality.
Simulation results against a 3GPP TR 38.901 Urban Macro channel with
calibrated bursty traffic show that the proposed agent achieves
\textbf{75--76\% power reduction} relative to always-on operation
while maintaining \textbf{90--94\% throughput efficiency} and
near-zero latency violations ($<$1\%) under pedestrian and urban
mobility scenarios.
This represents an \textbf{18--19 percentage-point improvement} in
power saving over the 3GPP Rel-18 Discontinuous Reception (DRX) baseline
with comparable QoS, and provides a direct pathway to 6G RAN
standardisation under 3GPP Rel-21 AI/ML-native UE autonomy study items.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
6G NR, energy efficiency, reinforcement learning, BWP-lite, wake-up
receiver, power saving, UE autonomy, Dueling DQN, 3GPP
\end{IEEEkeywords}

%% ========================= I. INTRODUCTION ================================ %%
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Energy consumption has emerged as a first-class design objective for the
sixth-generation (6G) wireless standard.
The IMT-2030 framework explicitly targets a $100\times$ improvement in
network energy efficiency relative to IMT-2020~\cite{itu_imt2030},
and the 3GPP 6G workshop has identified UE-side energy saving as a
critical enabler for sustainable mobile broadband reliable low-latency
communication (MBRLLC) and multi-purpose Communications, Computing,
Control, Localization and Sensing (3CLS) services~\cite{3gpp_6g_workshop}.

Existing 5G NR power-saving mechanisms, standardised in 3GPP Releases 16
through 18, rely on timer-based Discontinuous Reception (\drx{}) cycles,
static Bandwidth Part (\bwp{}) configurations, and Wake-Up Signal (WUS)
procedures.
These mechanisms have well-known limitations: \drx{} timers are configured
by the network without knowledge of short-term UE traffic state,
\bwp{} switching is triggered by inactivity thresholds rather than
predictive load estimates, and the WUS detection threshold is fixed at
deployment, regardless of channel conditions.
As a result, the UE either wastes power by remaining active during idle
periods, or degrades QoS by sleeping during traffic bursts.

The emerging 6G NR standard proposes three new primitives specifically
designed to address these limitations:
(i)~\textbf{BWP-lite}, a reduced-bandwidth operating mode that lowers
baseband and RF chain power by up to $5\times$ relative to full
operation~\cite{qualcomm_6g_proposals};
(ii)~\textbf{Wake-Up Receiver (\wur{})}, a low-power secondary receiver
($<$3~mW) that monitors for downlink activity while the main radio
sleeps~\cite{3gpp_tr38875};
and (iii)~\textbf{flexible \ssb{} periodicity}, allowing synchronisation
bursts at 5, 10, 20, or 40~ms intervals to reduce decoding overhead
during deep idle.
However, 3GPP Rel-19/20 study items specify these primitives
independently, with separate rule-based control loops.
The joint optimisation of all three parameters as a function of
instantaneous traffic load, channel quality, and buffer state has not
been addressed.

\textbf{Contributions.} This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We formulate the joint control of BWP mode, \wur{} detection
        threshold, and \ssb{} periodicity as a 14,400-state discrete-time
        MDP with a reward function explicitly designed to balance energy
        efficiency and latency QoS.
  \item We derive a statistical energy detector model for \wur{} miss-detection
        and false-alarm probabilities as a function of channel SNR and
        threshold level, and integrate this model directly into the RL
        reward to penalise inappropriate threshold selection.
  \item We train and evaluate a Dueling DQN agent with Prioritised
        Experience Replay (PER) against a 3GPP TR 38.901 Urban Macro
        channel model and a calibrated MMPP-2 bursty traffic source,
        demonstrating 75--76\% power saving with $<$1\% latency violations.
  \item We position our results against the 3GPP Rel-18 DRX baseline,
        an always-on policy, and a random policy, providing a complete
        performance characterisation across three UE mobility scenarios.
\end{itemize}

The rest of the paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work.
Section~\ref{sec:system} presents the system model.
Section~\ref{sec:mdp} formulates the MDP.
Section~\ref{sec:algo} describes the Dueling DQN algorithm.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents simulation results.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

%% ========================= II. RELATED WORK =============================== %%
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{3GPP UE Power Saving}

UE power saving in 5G NR has been standardised incrementally across
Releases 16--18.
Release 16 introduced C-DRX enhancements and \bwp{}-based power saving,
whereby the UE operates on a narrow \bwp{} during low-traffic periods to
reduce baseband power~\cite{3gpp_ts38300}.
Release 17 introduced the Wake-Up Signal (WUS) framework, allowing
the network to pre-signal UE activity before DRX on-durations,
reducing unnecessary wake-ups~\cite{3gpp_tr38875}.
Release 18 extended WUS to cross-slot scheduling and added enhanced
\drx{} adaptation~\cite{3gpp_rel18_ps}.
The 3GPP Rel-19 study item on 6G UE power saving (RP-251033) identifies
BWP-lite, \wur{} hardware, and flexible \ssb{} as candidate features,
but leaves joint control as an open problem.

\subsection{Machine Learning for UE Power Saving}

Several works have applied reinforcement learning to \drx{} optimisation.
\c{C}om\c{s}a \textit{et al.}~\cite{comsa2020} proposed a Q-learning
approach for DRX cycle adaptation in LTE, demonstrating reduced power
consumption at the cost of increased state complexity.
Cui \textit{et al.}~\cite{cui2022} applied Deep Q-Networks to \bwp{}
switching in 5G NR, optimising throughput under power constraints, but
did not model \wur{} or \ssb{} interactions.
More recently, several works~\cite{zhang2023,lee2024} have targeted
base station sleep mode scheduling using actor-critic methods, but the
UE-side control problem with hardware-level \wur{} statistics has received
limited attention.

\subsection{Positioning of This Work}

The key distinctions of our work are:
(i)~\textbf{Joint optimisation} of three interdependent 6G-specific
    primitives (BWP-lite, \wur{} threshold, \ssb{} period) in a single
    RL agent, rather than independent control loops;
(ii)~\textbf{Statistical \wur{} model} integrated into the reward,
    connecting physical-layer detection theory to MAC-layer control;
(iii)~\textbf{6G standardisation alignment}, with all parameters
    traceable to active 3GPP Rel-19/20 study items, making results
    directly relevant to ongoing standardisation discussions.

%% ========================= III. SYSTEM MODEL ============================== %%
\section{System Model}
\label{sec:system}

\subsection{UE Power Consumption Model}

We adopt the component-level power model from 3GPP TR~38.840.
The instantaneous UE power is decomposed as:
\begin{equation}
  P_{\mathrm{UE}}(t) = P_{\mathrm{PA}}(t) + P_{\mathrm{BB}}(t)
                     + P_{\mathrm{RF}}(t) + P_{\mathrm{WUR}}(t)
                     + P_{\mathrm{static}},
  \label{eq:power_model}
\end{equation}
where each component is a function of the BWP mode indicator
$b(t) \in \{0,1\}$ (0 = BWP-lite, 1 = full bandwidth) and WUR state
$w(t) \in \{0,1\}$.

\textbf{Power Amplifier:}
\begin{equation}
  P_{\mathrm{PA}}(t) = \left(1 - (1-\beta_{\mathrm{PA}}) \cdot \bar{b}(t)\right)
                       \frac{p_{tx}}{\eta_{\mathrm{PA}}},
  \label{eq:pa}
\end{equation}
where $\beta_{\mathrm{PA}} = \kappa \cdot \eta^{\mathrm{full}}/\eta^{\mathrm{lite}}
\approx 0.16$ is the BWP-lite PA power reduction factor,
$\kappa = 0.2$ is the bandwidth ratio, and $\bar{b}(t) = 1 - b(t)$.

\textbf{Baseband:}
\begin{equation}
  P_{\mathrm{BB}}(t) = P_{\mathrm{BB}}^{0}
    + \left(1 - (1-\kappa)\bar{b}(t)\right)
      \left(P_{\mathrm{BB}}^{\mathrm{full}} - P_{\mathrm{BB}}^{0}\right),
  \label{eq:bb}
\end{equation}
with static baseband floor $P_{\mathrm{BB}}^{0} = 30$~mW and full-load
$P_{\mathrm{BB}}^{\mathrm{full}} = 180$~mW.

\textbf{RF Chains:}
For a 4T8R device, the RF chain power is:
\begin{equation}
  P_{\mathrm{RF}}(t) = \left(1 + (N_{\mathrm{chain}}-1) \cdot b(t)\right)
                        P_{\mathrm{chain}},
  \label{eq:rf}
\end{equation}
where $N_{\mathrm{chain}} = 4$ and $P_{\mathrm{chain}} = 52$~mW.
In BWP-lite mode, only a single chain is active, saving 156~mW.

\textbf{WUR:}
\begin{equation}
  P_{\mathrm{WUR}}(t) = w(t) P_{\mathrm{WUR}}^{\mathrm{rx}}
                      + (1-w(t)) P_{\mathrm{WUR}}^{\mathrm{sleep}},
  \label{eq:wur_power}
\end{equation}
with $P_{\mathrm{WUR}}^{\mathrm{rx}} = 2$~mW and
$P_{\mathrm{WUR}}^{\mathrm{sleep}} = 0.1$~mW.

The average additional power from periodic \ssb{} decoding with period
$T_{\ssb{}} \in \{5, 10, 20, 40\}$~ms is:
\begin{equation}
  \bar{P}_{\mathrm{SSB}} = \frac{t_{\mathrm{SSB}}}{T_{\mathrm{SSB}}}
                            P_{\mathrm{decode}},
  \label{eq:ssb_power}
\end{equation}
where $t_{\mathrm{SSB}} = 0.14$~ms and $P_{\mathrm{decode}} = 120$~mW,
yielding 3.36~mW at $T_{\mathrm{SSB}} = 5$~ms down to 0.42~mW at
$T_{\mathrm{SSB}} = 40$~ms.

\subsection{WUR Detection Model}

The gNB transmits a Wake-Up Signal (WUS) occupying $N_{\mathrm{PRB}} = 1$
physical resource block, modulated over a known sequence of
$L_{\mathrm{WUS}} = 64$ symbols.
The UE WUR correlates the received signal against the known sequence and
applies an energy detector with threshold $\xi_\theta = \theta \cdot N_0 L_{\mathrm{WUS}}$,
where $\theta \in \{1, 2, 3\}$ is the threshold level.

Under Rayleigh fading with average SNR $\bar{\gamma}$, the
\textbf{false alarm probability} is:
\begin{equation}
  P_{\mathrm{FA}}(\theta) = \frac{\Gamma(L_{\mathrm{WUS}},\, \theta L_{\mathrm{WUS}})}
                                  {\Gamma(L_{\mathrm{WUS}})},
  \label{eq:pfa}
\end{equation}
where $\Gamma(\cdot, \cdot)$ is the upper regularised incomplete gamma function.
The threshold levels are calibrated to target $P_{\mathrm{FA}} \in \{0.15, 0.02, 0.001\}$
for $\theta \in \{1, 2, 3\}$ respectively.

The \textbf{miss-detection probability}, averaged over Rayleigh fading, is:
\begin{equation}
  P_{\mathrm{MD}}(\theta, \bar{\gamma}) = 1 -
    \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} e^{-\lambda} \frac{\lambda^k}{k!}
    \cdot \frac{\Gamma(L + k,\, \theta L)}{\Gamma(L + k)},
  \label{eq:pmd}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda = L_{\mathrm{WUS}} \cdot \bar{\gamma}$ is the
non-centrality parameter.

for key SNR operating points.

\subsection{Channel Model}

We adopt the 3GPP TR 38.901 Urban Macro (UMa) model at $f_c = 3.5$~GHz.
Path loss in the NLoS case is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{PL}_{\mathrm{NLoS}} = \max\!\left(\mathrm{PL}_{\mathrm{LoS}},\;
    13.54 + 39.08\log_{10}(d_{\mathrm{3D}}) + 20\log_{10}(f_c)\right),
  \label{eq:pl}
\end{equation}
with shadow fading $\sigma_{\mathrm{SF}} = 6$~dB.
Small-scale fading follows a correlated Rayleigh process with temporal
autocorrelation $R_h(\Delta t) = J_0(2\pi f_d \Delta t)$, where
$f_d = v f_c / c$ is the maximum Doppler shift.
The coherence time $T_c = 0.423/f_d$ ranges from 43.5~ms at 3~km/h
to 2.2~ms at 60~km/h, motivating the three mobility scenarios evaluated.

The CQI Markov transition matrix is estimated empirically from 100,000
channel realisations, yielding a tridiagonal-dominant matrix with
mean diagonal entry 0.89 (v = 3~km/h), 0.78 (v = 10~km/h), and
0.44 (v = 60~km/h).

\subsection{Traffic Model}

UE downlink traffic follows a two-state Markov-Modulated Poisson Process
(MMPP-2), calibrated to the 3GPP TR 36.814 mixed video-streaming and
web-browsing reference traffic profile:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{Q}_{\mathrm{MMPP}} =
  \begin{pmatrix} -\mu_{LH} & \mu_{LH} \\ \mu_{HL} & -\mu_{HL} \end{pmatrix},
  \label{eq:mmpp}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda_L = 0.1$~Mbps, $\lambda_H = 18.75$~Mbps,
$\mu_{LH} = 1.25$~s$^{-1}$ (mean low-load duration 800~ms), and
$\mu_{HL} = 5.0$~s$^{-1}$ (mean high-load duration 200~ms).
The stationary mean offered load is 2.5~Mbps with burstiness index
$c_v^2 \approx 4.2$.

%% ========================= IV. MDP FORMULATION ============================ %%
\section{MDP Formulation}
\label{sec:mdp}

\subsection{State Space}

The UE control problem is modelled as a discrete-time MDP
$\mathcal{M} = (\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{P}, \mathcal{R}, \gamma)$.
The state vector at slot $t$ is:
\begin{equation}
  s_t = \left(\rho_t,\; q_t,\; \mathrm{CQI}_t,\; b_t,\; \tau_t\right),
  \label{eq:state}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_t \in [0,1]$ is the EWMA traffic load, $q_t$ is the
normalised RLC buffer occupancy, $\mathrm{CQI}_t \in \{1,\ldots,15\}$
is the channel quality indicator, $b_t \in \{0,1\}$ is the current BWP
mode, and $\tau_t$ is the idle timer (slots since last packet).
The total discrete state space size is
$|\mathcal{S}| = 10 \times 8 \times 15 \times 2 \times 6 = 14{,}400$.

\subsection{Action Space}

The joint action is a tuple:
\begin{equation}
  a_t = \left(b_{t+1},\; \theta_t^{\mathrm{WUR}},\; p_t^{\mathrm{SSB}}\right)
        \in \{0,1\} \times \{1,2,3\} \times \{5,10,20,40\},
  \label{eq:action}
\end{equation}
giving $|\mathcal{A}| = 2 \times 3 \times 4 = 24$ discrete actions.
A switching cost $\mu_s = 0.05$ is included in the reward to prevent
rapid BWP toggling (chattering).

\subsection{Reward Function}

The reward at slot $t$ is:
\begin{equation}
  r_t = w_p \cdot r_t^{\mathrm{save}}
      + w_q \cdot r_t^{\mathrm{tput}}
      - w_b \cdot r_t^{\mathrm{buf}}
      - w_\ell \cdot r_t^{\mathrm{lat}}
      - \mu_s \cdot \mathbb{1}[b_{t+1} \neq b_t],
  \label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
where all terms are normalised to $[0,1]$.

The \textbf{power-saving reward}:
\begin{equation}
  r_t^{\mathrm{save}} = \frac{P_{\mathrm{ref}} - P_{\mathrm{UE}}(t)}{P_{\mathrm{ref}}},
  \quad P_{\mathrm{ref}} = 975\;\mathrm{mW},
  \label{eq:r_save}
\end{equation}
rewards the agent for reducing power relative to the always-on baseline.

The \textbf{throughput reward}:
\begin{equation}
  r_t^{\mathrm{tput}} =
  \begin{cases}
    \min\!\left(R_t / \lambda_t,\, 1\right) & \lambda_t > 0.5\;\mathrm{Mbps} \\
    1 & \text{otherwise (idle)}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:r_tput}
\end{equation}
rewards serving the offered load when traffic is present.

The \textbf{buffer penalty}:
\begin{equation}
  r_t^{\mathrm{buf}} = \min\!\left(q_t / q_{\mathrm{sat}},\, 1\right),
  \quad q_{\mathrm{sat}} = 0.1\;\mathrm{MB},
  \label{eq:r_buf}
\end{equation}
penalises buffer build-up, creating a strong gradient toward wake-up
during traffic bursts. This is the key term that prevents the
over-sleeping failure mode.

The \textbf{latency penalty}:
\begin{equation}
  r_t^{\mathrm{lat}} = \min\!\left(D_t / (2 D_{\mathrm{max}}),\, 1\right),
  \quad D_{\mathrm{max}} = 10\;\mathrm{ms},
  \label{eq:r_lat}
\end{equation}
applies a soft barrier around the 6G URLLC latency target.

The weight vector $(w_p, w_q, w_b, w_\ell) = (0.35, 0.35, 0.55, 0.20)$
is tuned so that the expected reward under deep-sleep-when-idle
behaviour ($\approx 0.56$) exceeds the always-on reward ($\approx 0.35$),
while sleeping during active traffic incurs a net negative reward
($\approx -0.20$), forcing the agent to learn traffic-responsive
wake-up behaviour.

%% ========================= V. ALGORITHM =================================== %%
\section{Dueling DQN with Prioritised Replay}
\label{sec:algo}

\subsection{Network Architecture}

We employ a Dueling DQN~\cite{wang2016dueling} that factorises the
action-value function as:
\begin{equation}
  Q(s,a;\theta) = V(s;\theta_V)
    + \left(A(s,a;\theta_A)
      - \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|}\sum_{a'}A(s,a';\theta_A)\right),
  \label{eq:dueling}
\end{equation}
separating the state value $V(s)$ from the action advantage $A(s,a)$.
This decomposition accelerates convergence in idle states where most
actions have similar value, allowing the value stream to update from
all transitions regardless of which action was taken.

The network architecture is:
\begin{equation*}
  \mathbb{R}^5 \xrightarrow{\mathrm{FC}_{64},\,\mathrm{ReLU}}
  \mathbb{R}^{64} \xrightarrow{\mathrm{FC}_{64},\,\mathrm{ReLU}}
  \mathbb{R}^{64} \xrightarrow{\mathrm{split}}
  \begin{cases} V: \mathbb{R}^1 \\ A: \mathbb{R}^{24} \end{cases}
\end{equation*}
with 5,953 total parameters — well within UE on-device inference budgets,
requiring $<$0.1~mW of additional power at a 1~kHz decision rate.

\subsection{Prioritised Experience Replay}

We use Prioritised Experience Replay (PER)~\cite{schaul2016per} to
oversample high-TD-error transitions, which in our setting correspond
to BWP switching decisions at traffic burst boundaries.
The sampling probability for transition $i$ is:
\begin{equation}
  P(i) = \frac{(\delta_i + \varepsilon)^\alpha}
              {\sum_j (\delta_j + \varepsilon)^\alpha},
  \quad \alpha = 0.6,\; \varepsilon = 0.01,
  \label{eq:per}
\end{equation}
with importance sampling correction weight
$w_i \propto (N \cdot P(i))^{-\beta}$, $\beta$ annealed from 0.4 to 1.0.

\subsection{Training Configuration}

The agent is trained for 500,000 steps using the Adam optimiser with
learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$, batch size 256, and replay buffer
capacity 100,000.
The target network is updated via soft copy with $\tau = 0.005$.
Exploration follows an $\epsilon$-greedy schedule decaying from 1.0 to
0.05 over the first 10\% of training steps.

\section{Simulation Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Simulation Setup}

We evaluate the proposed agent against four baseline policies:
\textbf{Always-on} ($b=1$, $\theta=1$, $T_{\mathrm{SSB}}=5$~ms),

$T_{\mathrm{DRX}}=40$~ms), \textbf{Random} (uniform over $\mathcal{A}$),
and a \textbf{Value Iteration Oracle} (offline, requires future traffic knowledge).
Each policy is evaluated over 20 episodes of 5,000 slots (5 seconds)
at three UE velocities: $v \in \{3, 10, 60\}$~km/h.
All simulations use a fixed gNB transmit power of 43~dBm,
UE-gNB distance of 200~m, and the hardware profile in Table~\ref{tab:hw}.

uniform action selection reduces power by only 40.7\% with 2.1\%
latency violations, significantly worse than the learned policy.

\subsection{Action Distribution Analysis}

Post-training analysis of the learned policy reveals three distinct
behavioural modes:
(i)~\textbf{Deep idle} ($\rho < 0.1$, $\tau > 100$~ms): agent selects
BWP-lite with $\theta = 3$ and $T_{\mathrm{SSB}} = 40$~ms 87\% of the time;
(ii)~\textbf{Active traffic} ($\rho > 0.5$): agent switches to full-BW
within 2--3 slots of traffic onset, enabled by the buffer penalty gradient;
(iii)~\textbf{Transition} ($0.1 \leq \rho \leq 0.5$): agent uses BWP-lite
with $\theta = 1$ (low miss-detection risk) and $T_{\mathrm{SSB}} = 10$~ms,
maintaining rapid wake-up capability while preserving partial power saving.
This three-mode behaviour is precisely the adaptive strategy that
rule-based DRX cannot achieve with fixed timers.

%% ========================= VII. CONCLUSION ================================ %%
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a Dueling DQN agent for joint optimisation of three
6G NR UE power-saving primitives: BWP-lite mode, WUR detection threshold,
and SSB periodicity.
The agent is trained in a 3GPP-compliant simulation environment with a
UMa channel model, calibrated bursty MMPP-2 traffic, and a component-level
UE power model traceable to TR 38.840.
A key contribution is the integration of a statistical WUR energy detector
model directly into the RL reward, enabling the agent to learn
threshold-selection policies that adapt to instantaneous channel quality.

Experimental results show 75--76\% power saving relative to always-on
operation under pedestrian and urban mobility, representing an 18--19
percentage-point improvement over the 3GPP Rel-18 DRX baseline,
with throughput efficiency of 90--94\% and $<$1\% latency violations.
The lightweight network (5,953 parameters) is feasible for on-device
inference at $<$0.1~mW additional power, supporting deployment on
6G NR UE silicon.

Future work will extend the framework to multi-cell scenarios with
inter-cell interference, incorporate UE velocity as an explicit state
variable for vehicular robustness, and explore the connection to
3GPP Rel-21 AI/ML-native RAN proposals for standardisation.
